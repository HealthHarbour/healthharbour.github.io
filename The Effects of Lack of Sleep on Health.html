The Effects of Lack of Sleep on Health

Obesity

Not getting enough sleep is linked to fat. Insufficient sleep is associated with higher levels of the hormone that increases appetite, and lower levels of the hormone that leads to feeling less full. This sets people up for a build-up of fats in the body. Lack of sleep is associated with fat accumulation. Lack of sleep is one of the largest risk factors of obesity. In a short study on sleep duration, researchers found that adults not getting enough sleep are 55% more likely to be obese. Even worse, children and teenagers are 89% more susceptible to obesity with insufficient sleep.

Changes in Hormones

Sleep regulates the level of cortisol, also known as the stress hormone. Cortisol levels are lowered with the right amount of sleep. Elevated levels of this hormone over time is associated with many risk factors. When you get a good night’s sleep and wake up feeling restored, the levels of more helpful hormones associated with natural energy increase.

The Immune System

Another health benefit of sleep is helping fight off sickness. In a research study, sleep deprivation has been linked to stomach and intestine disorders that can lead to nausea. Infection-fighting antibodies and cells are reduced during periods when you don't get enough sleep and as a result, your body is more susceptible to sickness.

Learning Patterns

Sleep is crucial for learning and memory formation. Sleep deprivation can lead to problems like decreased attention. Concentration is vital to learning and academic success, but inadequate sleep reduces attention and focus. A good night's sleep will help you retain new information. Sleep enables the brain to strengthen neural connections by replaying memories from the day.

Depression

Short sleep increases the genetic risk of developing depression. In fact, it has been estimated that 90% of patients with depression also suffer from some form of sleep apnea, which is a very common condition in which your breathing stops and restarts many times while you sleep. Furthermore, many different mechanisms have been proposed to justify the likelihood of developing depression. Let’s start by thinking about our behaviour. We may be more likely to cancel an evening out or an exercise session after a poor night’s sleep. Perhaps this is part of the problem, as such events may keep depressive symptoms at bay.
